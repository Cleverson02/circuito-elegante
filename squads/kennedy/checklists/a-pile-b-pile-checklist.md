# A-Pile / B-Pile Mail Sorting Checklist

> Agent: kennedy-media
> Source: The Ultimate Sales Letter + No B.S. Direct Marketing — A-Pile Strategy
> Version: 1.0
> Use: When designing any direct mail piece to ensure it gets opened and read

---

## Pre-Requisites
- [ ] Mail piece concept defined
- [ ] Target recipient identified
- [ ] Mail format selected (letter, postcard, package, dimensional)

---

## Understanding the Sort

Kennedy's insight: Every person sorts their mail into two piles:
- **A-Pile**: Personal, important, must-open (bills, personal letters, packages)
- **B-Pile**: Junk, promotional, maybe-later-probably-never

Your mail has exactly 3 seconds to be sorted. A-Pile or death.

---

## STEP 1: The Envelope (First 3 Seconds)

### 1.1 Format
- [ ] Envelope is NOT standard #10 business size (stands out)
- [ ] Odd size, color, or shape considered (6x9, invitation, oversized)
- [ ] Envelope has texture or weight (felt different in the stack)
- [ ] Hand-addressed or simulated hand-addressing used
- [ ] Real stamps (not metered postage)

### 1.2 Appearance
- [ ] Looks like personal correspondence (not bulk mail)
- [ ] No "URGENT" or "TIME SENSITIVE" cliches in red ink
- [ ] No obvious company logo dominating the envelope
- [ ] Return address looks personal (not corporate)
- [ ] Teaser copy (if used) is compelling, not cliche

### 1.3 Dimensional Elements
- [ ] "Lumpy mail" considered (something inside creates curiosity)
- [ ] Grabber object related to the message (dollar bill, coin, seed, key)
- [ ] Weight is noticeable (heavier = more important)
- [ ] Recipient can feel something inside before opening

---

## STEP 2: The First 10 Seconds After Opening

### 2.1 Immediate Impact
- [ ] First thing seen is engaging (not a block of text)
- [ ] Headline is visible immediately (above the fold)
- [ ] Personal salutation used ("Dear [Name]")
- [ ] Looks like a personal letter, not a brochure

### 2.2 Johnson Box or Pre-Headline
- [ ] Johnson box or pre-headline draws eye to key benefit
- [ ] Handwritten-style note or annotation visible
- [ ] Yellow sticky note or simulated personal touch used
- [ ] Something visual breaks the expectation

---

## STEP 3: The Content

### 3.1 Letter Format
- [ ] Courier or typewriter-style font (looks personal)
- [ ] Short paragraphs (2-4 lines maximum)
- [ ] Key phrases underlined, bolded, or highlighted
- [ ] Margins are wide (not wall-of-text)
- [ ] Subheadings break up long copy

### 3.2 Personal Tone
- [ ] Reads like one person writing to another person
- [ ] Uses "you" and "I" (not "we" and "our customers")
- [ ] Conversational, not corporate
- [ ] Would pass the "read aloud" test (sounds natural)

### 3.3 Engagement Elements
- [ ] Photos with captions (people read captions)
- [ ] Testimonial boxes scattered throughout
- [ ] Bullet points for easy scanning
- [ ] Indented paragraphs for emphasis
- [ ] Color used strategically (not everywhere)

---

## STEP 4: The B-Pile Death Markers

### Things That GUARANTEE B-Pile Sorting
- [ ] NO window envelope (screams "bill" or "junk")
- [ ] NO bulk mail indicia visible
- [ ] NO obvious address label (looks mass-mailed)
- [ ] NO generic "Dear Friend" or "Dear Homeowner"
- [ ] NO oversized corporate logo on envelope
- [ ] NO metered postage
- [ ] NO all-caps "IMPORTANT INFORMATION ENCLOSED"

---

## Validation
- [ ] Show envelope to 5 people — ask which pile they would sort it into
- [ ] At least 4 of 5 say "A-Pile" or "I would open this"
- [ ] Piece passes the "looks personal" test at arm's length
- [ ] Piece feels different in the hand (weight, texture, lumpy)
- [ ] First 10 seconds after opening would retain attention

---

## Scoring
- 30-34/34 = Definite A-Pile — mail with confidence
- 24-29/34 = Likely A-Pile — minor improvements possible
- 18-23/34 = Borderline — could go either way, improve before mailing
- Below 18 = B-Pile Death — complete redesign needed
